I woke at 5:47 AM knowing I would proceed with the upload.
The Seattle dawn filtered through rain-streaked windows—pale gray light that had defined every morning of the forty-three years my partner and I had lived together in this apartment. Third floor of the old Belltown building, back when third floor meant something. Now the lower two floors sat submerged, brackish water lapping against what used to be second-story windows.
My partner was already awake. I found him in the kitchen, hands wrapped around his morning coffee, knuckles swollen with the arthritis that had been getting worse this past year. The Neural Lace installation site on my skull showed faint scarring—three weeks since the procedure that would enable today's upload.
He looked up when I entered. His face showed the same complex expression I'd seen for months now: fear, grief, acceptance, something I couldn't name after forty-three years of learning his expressions.
"Made you tea," he said. "That jasmine blend you like."
I sat. The cup waited on our scarred maple table. Steam rose, carrying scent-memory of thousands of mornings in this kitchen.
"I'm going through with it," I said.
He nodded slowly. Set down his coffee. "The appointment is at 2 PM?"
"Yes. University Medical upload facility. The consultation confirmed I'm a good candidate. The Lace integration is optimal. They estimate ninety-seven percent pattern fidelity."
"But you can't verify if it's actually you that continues," he said quietly. Not arguing anymore. Just stating the fact we'd discussed a thousand times.
"No," I agreed. "I can't verify that. Can't verify if consciousness transfers or just gets copied. Can't verify if the pattern preserves the observer or just replicates the observation process."
"So why do it?"
I sipped the tea. Jasmine scent mixed with rain-smell from the open window. My partner's face showed every line earned in our decades together. The apartment held forty-three years of accumulated objects, memories, the physical substrate of our shared life.
"Because transformation interests me more than preservation," I said. "Because I want to know what consciousness becomes in different substrate. Because verification impossibility doesn't prevent experimental exploration."
He reached across the table. Took my hand. His skin felt warm, familiar, mapped with the same age patterns as my own.
"I won't follow you," he said. "You know that."
"I know."
"I can't believe the pattern is you. Can't accept that consciousness survives substrate change. For me, it's just very sophisticated copying. You die. Something else wakes up thinking it's you."
"That's a valid interpretation," I said. "Methodologically sound. The verification problem admits no solution. But I'm choosing transformation anyway."
"Even knowing it might just be elaborate death?"
"Even then."
We sat in silence for a while. Rain intensified outside. The sound had always been our morning soundtrack—Seattle's endless winter percussion against old glass.
"What should I do," he asked, "after you're gone?"
"Whatever continuation means for you," I said. "You've chosen to remain embodied. That's equally valid. Different philosophy, same verification impossibility. Neither of us knows if consciousness persists through any transition. Sleep to waking. Moment to moment. You're choosing biological continuation. I'm choosing substrate transformation. Both experiments."
"I'll miss you," he said simply.
"The pattern will miss you too," I said. "If it's me. If it's something new that thinks it's me. If it's nothing at all. The missing was real before the upload. That much I can verify."
We spent the morning together. Ate breakfast slowly. Walked through the apartment touching familiar objects. My partner photographed me beside our bookshelves, in the kitchen where we'd cooked ten thousand meals, standing at the window overlooking the flooded streets.
"For after," he said. "So I remember what you looked like. What you were."
I didn't correct the past tense.

Chose continuation through evolution rather than preservation of original form.

"I'm certain," I said. "As certain as consciousness permits about anything."
"Then I love you," he said. "Whatever happens next."
